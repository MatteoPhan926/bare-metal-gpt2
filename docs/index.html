A GPT-2 inference engine, written from scratch in C and CUDA

Pure C → CUDA · five optimization stages · every number gated, roofline-checked, and reproducible
One model (GPT-2-124M), one GPU (RTX 4060 Laptop, sm_89).

Code Benchmarks Roofline Design log

Abstract

A single-model, forward-inference-only engine for GPT-2-124M: first a pure-C fp32 forward pass with no dependencies, then a CUDA port, then five stages of optimization applied one at a time. Every stage is gated against a HuggingFace reference before it is timed, every number is checked against a roofline ceiling measured on the actual GPU, and every stage that failed to pay for itself is still in the repository, with the measurement that killed it.

There is no novelty claim. The claim is that the numbers are true, and that each one can be traced to where it came from.

Where it lands

Same GPU, same weights, same session, matched precision, medians (never best-of-N). Decode is true M=1 in all three engines. PyTorch prefill is measured on the transformer trunk only, because HF's model(ids) also runs the LM head over all P positions and this engine does not.

Decode runs at 76–83% of llama.cpp and 4.2–4.8× faster than PyTorch eager. Prefill is 10–14× slower than llama.cpp.

llama.cpp is faster, and the gap is worth explaining rather than burying. At P=512 the 14.44× decomposes into two measured factors: 

14.44x  =  3.12x   tensor cores (WMMA)
                   31.51 TFLOP/s measured tensor-GEMM ceiling
                 / 10.10 TFLOP/s measured achievable CUDA-core GEMM ceiling
        x  4.63x   GEMM maturity
                   llama.cpp reaches 39.3% of its ceiling; this engine reaches 8.5% of its own

The first factor is a ceiling this engine cannot reach, because it uses no tensor cores — a scope decision, not an oversight. It is the ratio of two independently microbenchmarked peaks. The second is the distance between a textbook 16×16 shared-memory tile and a register-tiled, double-buffered, vectorized GEMM; it is the residual, and it equals the ratio of how much of its own ceiling each engine reaches. Together the two measured factors account for the full gap.

Fusion is not part of the answer. Attention is 5.3% of prefill after Stage 3b, and the ~123 kernel launches in a prefill forward cost a small fraction of it, well under 1% — the per-op profile's sum-of-parts lands within 0.7% of the uninstrumented forward, which bounds the per-kernel overhead from above.

One number is worth reading twice: PyTorch eager's trunk prefill is 9.441 ms at P=128 and 9.395 ms at P=512 — identical, for four times the work. It is host-dispatch-bound at this model size, which is why beating it on decode is table stakes and llama.cpp is the only baseline that says anything.

The ladder

Each stage changes one thing, re-passes the correctness gates, then is timed against the previous stage back-to-back on the same thermal state. A stage that doesn't earn its keep is reverted. Decode before Stage 5 is a no-KV full recompute (M≫1, prefill-shaped) — a true M=1 decode does not exist in this engine until the KV cache lands.

What it taught

A kernel can be compute-bound on work it throws away

Stage 4's INT8 halves the weight bytes and buys exactly 1.00×, at every shape. Two falsifiable predictions pinned down why. If the M=1 head GEMV were bandwidth-bound, halving its bytes gives ~2× — measured 1.002×, rejected. If it were compute-bound on the tile's discarded rows, then M=1 and M=16 cost the same — measured 1.4351 ms vs 1.4346 ms, confirmed. The kernel runs at 860.6 GFLOP/s, 98.8% of its own M=512 throughput, while pulling 23% of achieved bandwidth. Fewer bytes cannot help a kernel that isn't waiting on bytes.

INT8's decode payoff is bounded by the quality gate, not the hardware

Even after Stage 5 gave it a KV cache and a proper GEMV, INT8 buys 1.02–1.16×. The tied output head is 31% of the weight bytes and must stay fp16 or the quality gate fails; the 48 quantizable block matmuls are 1.2–4.7 MB each, small enough to sit on a per-kernel latency floor at M=1. The weight-side ceiling on the whole-step speedup is 1.278×. The roofline's director map predicted "high" — the honest answer is that it is high for the models that prediction was written about, and GPT-2-124M is too small. A property of the model, not a failure of the engine.

At batch=1 a 124M model is launch-bound, not bandwidth-bound

Independently confirmed by the baseline. This engine's decode step reaches 127.4 GB/s (54.6% of copy bandwidth); llama.cpp, with CUDA graphs and fused kernels, reaches 159.6 GB/s (68.4%). Both are far from the bus. The roofline said decode was memory-bound; at this model size, what it actually waits on is kernel launches. The decode gap runs 1.24× at ctx=128 — almost entirely that fixed per-step overhead — rising to 1.31× at ctx=1023, where the fixed cost is still 73% of the gap and the remaining 27% is our attention kernel scaling worse with context.

I checked my own denominators, and one was wrong

For several stages the CUDA-core compute ceiling was derived from the clock (24 SM × 128 lanes × 2 × 2.61 GHz = 16.0 TFLOP/s) and then used as the denominator for "% of roofline". Measuring it showed the achievable non-tensor GEMM is 10.10 TFLOP/s — the assumption was optimistic by 1.55×, and the ridge point it implied (≈69) was wrong; the real one is 43.3. Labelling a number "derived, not measured" does not license using it as a measurement. The correction is in the git history, along with a clock64() cross-check that reported 146% of the hardware's issue width — impossible — and was discarded rather than published.

Scope

Forward inference, batch=1, one model, one GPU, the ladder above. Not a framework, not a serving system, not multi-GPU, not a llama.cpp clone. Tensor cores (WMMA), kernel fusion beyond Stage 3, and CUDA graph capture are named in the roofline as the levers that would close the remaining gap — and are deliberately not built, which is precisely why the gap can be attributed to them rather than hand-waved.

BENCHMARKS.md also records an INT8-vs-llama.cpp-Q8_0 decode comparison, deliberately not headlined: the schemes don't match. Ours is mixed precision — 48 block matmuls in INT8 with the tied head held in fp16 because the quality gate demands it — while Q8_0 is uniform int8 over every weight. Different byte counts, different quality points, no Δppl measured for Q8_0. Context, not a result.
